"Sales & Operations Planning: The How‑To Handbook" is one of the classic practitioner guides for designing and implementing an S&OP process that actually works in a real company, not just on a slide.
## What this handbook is
- It is a practical implementation guide for S&OP, focused on how to set up the executive process, roles, meetings, and spreadsheets rather than on theory.
- The author walks through composition of the Executive S&OP team, meeting cadence, data requirements, and low‑cost spreadsheet-based designs before you ever touch big S&OP software.
- It targets companies that either (a) are implementing S&OP for the first time or (b) have a weak "demand/supply meeting" and want to harden it into a disciplined, executive‑level process.
## Core ideas (in plain language)
- S&OP is a structured, monthly, cross‑functional decision process to balance demand and supply over a 12–24‑month horizon, at product‑family level, tied to the financial plan.
- It links three layers: strategy (3–5 years), S&OP (2–18 months), and detailed scheduling (0–6 weeks), ensuring that long‑term targets and constraints are reflected in mid‑term volume plans and short‑term execution.
- The process is owned by the executive team, not by planning; planners prepare scenarios and numbers, executives make trade‑off decisions (service vs inventory vs capacity vs margin).
This is why the book invests a lot of space in "who is in the room", agenda design, and how to keep the conversation at the right level of aggregation.
## Typical S&OP process the book supports
The handbook aligns with the standard 4–5 step S&OP cycle, with heavy emphasis on governance and templates.
1. Demand planning
   - Build a rolling 18‑month unconstrained demand forecast at product‑family level using history plus commercial intelligence.
   - Review assumptions (promotions, new launches, cannibalization, channel shifts) and measure forecast accuracy over time.
2. Supply planning
   - Translate the consensus demand into capacity and material requirements at an aggregated level.
   - Identify constraints (lines, labor, key RM, suppliers) and build scenarios: add overtime, change mix, push out demand, use external capacity.
3. Pre‑S&OP (balancing)
   - Cross‑functional team (sales, marketing, operations, supply chain, finance) reconciles demand and supply options, aligns with financial targets, and prepares 2–3 options for executives.
   - Key outputs: recommended volume plan, inventory trajectory, service implications, and financial impact versus budget.
4. Executive S&OP meeting
   - Executive team reviews options, decides on one "single set of numbers," and approves major trade‑offs.
   - Decisions are documented, communicated, and become the official plan for operations, sales, and finance.
5. Execution and continuous improvement
   - KPI tracking on forecast accuracy, OTIF, inventory, capacity utilization, and adherence to the S&OP plan.
   - Use variance analysis and post‑mortems to improve assumptions and the process design itself.
## What you can expect to learn / use
- How S&OP works end‑to‑end: definition, objectives (service, cost, inventory, stability), and relationship to ERP and the broader supply chain.
- Implementation roadmap: how to start small, secure executive sponsorship, define scope, and avoid over‑engineering in early phases.
- Team and governance: how to select executive participants, define roles, design charters, and set a monthly cadence that people actually follow.
- Data and tools: how to structure S&OP spreadsheets, aggregate data, and connect with ERP without needing full‑blown integrated planning software at the start.
- Performance and improvement: how to choose and track KPIs, and how to systematically "make it better and better" once the basic cycle is running.
## How to apply it in practice
- Phase 1 – Concept and scope
  - Use the early chapters to frame S&OP's purpose for your executive team and agree on scope (which business units, which product families, which horizon).
  - Draft a one‑page S&OP charter: objectives, meeting cadence, participants, decision rights.
- Phase 2 – Pilot design
  - Borrow or adapt the handbook's spreadsheet structures for demand and supply reviews at family level.
  - Define a pilot (e.g., one division or region) and run 3–4 cycles focusing on discipline and learning, not perfection.
- Phase 3 – Institutionalize
  - Use the governance and KPI chapters to formalize roles, integrate with your budget/IBP cycle, and connect S&OP outputs into MRP, production scheduling, and sales target setting.
  - Gradually decide where specialized tools (APS, demand planning software) can replace or augment the initial spreadsheet approach.
